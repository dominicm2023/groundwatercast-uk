Winterbournes

When will the bourne flow?

Winterbournes are chalk streams that dry by design — flowing only when the aquifer beneath them is high enough. Their beds are dry for part of most years, and the question locals actually ask is not "how high is it?" but "when will it start — or stop?"

Each winterbourne's page shows whether it's flowing today, the months it typically runs dry, and the 14-day outlook for the groundwater-fed flow that decides it.

For abstraction & licensing work

Q95, Hands-off Flow, and your own trigger levels

Q95 as the reference
Each gauge's headline probability is P(flow < Q95 within 14 days) — Q95 computed from that gauge's own full record. It is a climatological proxy, not the Hands-off-Flow condition on any abstraction licence.
Gauged, not naturalised
We forecast the flow as measured at the gauge, including any abstraction and discharge effects upstream. Rating curves — the stage-to-flow conversion — are least accurate exactly where low-flow work cares most.
Your own trigger level
Licence holders watch conditions written in their own licence, not Q95. We're piloting custom trigger levels — your HoF value on your gauge's forecast. Get in touch →

Why rivers, here

Summer baseflow is groundwater

In a chalk stream, the water in July fell as rain months ago and has been draining through the aquifer since. That slow physics is why a 14-day flow forecast can work at all — and it's the same physics behind our borehole forecasts.
