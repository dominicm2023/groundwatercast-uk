How GroundwaterCast works

Per-borehole, probabilistic, and built entirely on open data.

⚠ Indicative, experimental — not a flood or drought warning. England only. An independent open-source project, not affiliated with or endorsed by any employer, the Environment Agency, ECMWF, or any water company.

One vocabulary, three horizons

Every monitored borehole in England is described the same way — below, near or above normal — across three time horizons, so a single glance means the same thing everywhere:

  • Current status vs normal. The latest observed level placed on the borehole's own monthly climatology — a percentile for the month — plus a 7-day trend. "Normal" is always relative to that specific borehole and that specific month of the year.
  • 14-day forecast. A P10 / P50 / P90 fan produced by driving the 51-member ECMWF ensemble through a recharge model calibrated to the borehole, together with the probability of breaching a threshold.
  • Seasonal outlook (1–6 months). Tercile probabilities of below / near / above-normal groundwater from a SEAS5-weighted ensemble of plausible weather traces. Experimental.

A data-driven approach

Each borehole's forecast comes from a model calibrated to that borehole's own record — learning how its water level responds to rainfall and evaporation — rather than a detailed physical model of the aquifer built site by site. That deliberate trade, breadth over physical detail, is what lets a single open, reproducible method run across every monitored borehole in England. It complements detailed local groundwater studies rather than replacing them.

The pipeline runs daily: it pulls the latest Environment Agency readings and open weather forcing, updates each borehole's status against its climatology, and regenerates the 14-day fan and seasonal outlook.

How far ahead the seasonal outlook means anything

The 14-day forecast and the 6-month seasonal outlook rely on different things. Detailed weather is only predictable about two weeks ahead — beyond that the atmosphere is effectively chaotic, and the UK is one of the harder places in the world for seasonal rainfall forecasts. So any honest long-range outlook has to fade towards the historical climatology — the normal range for the time of year. A seasonal forecast that stayed confident would simply be overconfident.

Groundwater, though, has a long memory: the level you see today already reflects months — sometimes years — of past rainfall. So the outlook isn't reverting to a blank average, but to climatology conditioned on where the borehole sits right now. A borehole that is low today tends to stay relatively low for a while almost regardless of the weather, and that starting state is the main source of any real skill — not the seasonal weather model, which only nudges the odds slightly over the first few months.

In practice: months 1–2 carry genuine information from the current level; by months 4–6 the outlook is closer to "the seasonal range for a borehole starting in this condition." Slow aquifers such as the deep chalk hold that information for longer than fast, flashy boreholes. This is why the seasonal outlook is shown as probabilities rather than a single line, and labelled experimental.

Everything is open

GroundwaterCast uses only free, openly-licensed inputs, and the code is open source — so the forecasts are reproducible end to end.

  • Environment Agency hydrology — observed groundwater levels and rainfall (Open Government Licence v3.0).
  • ECMWF Open Data — the 15-day ensemble forcing (CC-BY-4.0).
  • Copernicus ERA5 / SEAS5 — reanalysis history and seasonal ensemble (modified Copernicus Climate Change Service information).
  • BGS 625k bedrock geology — indicative aquifer designation (OGL).
  • Source code — free and open source (MIT), on GitHub.

What this is — and isn't

GroundwaterCast is an indicative, uncalibrated research forecast. The uncertainty bands describe how the model's plausible futures spread, not a formally validated probability. It covers England only, and it is not a flood or drought warning and must not be used for safety-critical or operational decisions. For those, always rely on the Environment Agency and other official sources.

Of the roughly 1,378 monitored boreholes, a forecast tranche carries the full 14-day and seasonal outlook; the rest show current status where a recent reading exists.
